# Personalized Gut Health Analysis from Shotgun Metagenomic Sequencing

## Project Overview

The goal of this project is to build an end-to-end system that transforms raw shotgun metagenomic sequencing data into a personalized, easy-to-understand gut health report for non-technical users.

Rather than presenting users with complex microbial abundance tables, the system translates sequencing results into actionable insights supported by scientific literature. The emphasis is on personalization, explainability, and communicating biological concepts in a way that anyone can understand.

---

# Problem Statement

Most microbiome analysis tools are designed for researchers and clinicians. They often produce outputs that include:

* Thousands of microbial species
* Taxonomic abundance tables
* Complex statistical metrics
* Scientific terminology unfamiliar to consumers

For the average person, these reports provide little practical value.

This project aims to bridge that gap by creating an AI-powered interpreter that explains a person's gut microbiome in simple language while grounding every recommendation in scientific evidence.

---

# Objectives

The system should:

* Accept a shotgun metagenomic stool sample (FASTQ)
* Process the sequencing data using standard metagenomic pipelines
* Identify microbial composition
* Retrieve relevant scientific literature
* Generate personalized interpretations based on the individual's profile
* Present results using intuitive visualizations and plain English

# Data Source

Only a single public shotgun metagenomic stool sample is required for the prototype.

Potential datasets include:

* NCBI Sequence Read Archive (SRA)
* European Nucleotide Archive (ENA)
* Human Microbiome Project (HMP)
* MG-RAST

A synthetic microbiome profile may also be created for demonstration purposes if processing a full FASTQ file is outside the project's scope.

# Pipeline

```
FASTQ

↓

Quality Control
(FastQC)

↓

Read Trimming
(fastp)

↓

Taxonomic Classification
(Kraken2 / MetaPhlAn)

↓

Relative Species Abundance

↓

Scientific Literature Retrieval

↓

LLM Interpretation

↓

Personalized Report
```

# Example Personalized Output

## Susan's Gut Snapshot

Think of your gut as a city made up of trillions of tiny organisms that help digest food, produce nutrients, and support your immune system.

Your results suggest:

* You have fewer beneficial fiber-digesting bacteria than expected.
* A key anti-inflammatory species, *Faecalibacterium prausnitzii*, appears to be relatively low.
* Your *E. coli* levels are slightly higher than average. Many strains are completely harmless, but elevated levels can sometimes be associated with dietary changes or recent antibiotic use.

---

## What This Means

Your microbiome currently appears better at processing proteins than plant fiber.

Because you've reported bloating, dramatically increasing fiber overnight may worsen symptoms initially.

Instead, gradually introducing foods such as:

* oats
* lentils
* beans
* asparagus

may help beneficial bacteria increase over time.

---

## Gut Garden Analogy

Imagine your gut as a garden.

Right now:

🌱 There are fewer flowers (beneficial bacteria).

🌿 A few weeds are growing more than we'd like.

Rather than removing bacteria, the goal is to feed the beneficial organisms so they naturally become more abundant.

# Scientific Literature Integration

For every interpretation, the system retrieves supporting evidence from peer-reviewed publications.

Example:

Finding:
Low abundance of *Faecalibacterium prausnitzii*

Supporting literature:

* Reduced abundance has been associated with inflammatory bowel disease.
* Produces butyrate, an important anti-inflammatory short-chain fatty acid.
* Often associated with high-fiber diets.

The report includes a short explanation alongside citations to the underlying research.

# Handling Unfamiliar Biological Data

One objective of this project is demonstrating the ability to rapidly learn a new scientific domain.

Approach:

1. Identify the standard workflow used in metagenomics.
2. Read foundational papers describing sequencing and taxonomic classification.
3. Learn commonly used analysis tools such as Kraken2 and MetaPhlAn.
4. Process a publicly available dataset.
5. Compare results against published benchmarks.
6. Build downstream AI applications on top of validated outputs.

This structured approach minimizes assumptions and ensures interpretations are grounded in established scientific methods.
